About TerraTech

Traditional
precision.
Modern intelligence.

TerraTech was founded in 1991 by a team of geodetic engineers who believed accurate spatial data was the foundation of every serious infrastructure decision. For the first decade we did exactly that — land surveying, control networks, and topographic mapping across Florida and the Gulf Coast.

Then something changed. The instruments got smarter, the satellites got cheaper, the data got bigger. We chose to modernise rather than stay comfortable. Today our work combines the precision of classical geodesy with orbital-scale remote sensing — a combination very few firms offer end-to-end.

“We still send people into the field with total stations. We also receive satellite imagery from 600 km altitude. Both are essential. Neither is enough alone.”

Project Highlights

Work from the field.

Three examples of what TerraTech actually does in practice — one from each sector. These are the kinds of projects you may be working on.

Sector 01 — Infrastructure
Bogotá, Colombia
Highway viaduct deformation monitoring, Bogotá ring road expansion
TerraTech Madrid and Bogotá offices established a geodetic control network across a 47-km highway corridor. Bi-annual drone LiDAR surveys track millimetre-level settlement in five bridge structures over a five-year construction and consolidation period. Data feeds directly into the client’s structural engineering platform via TerraCloud API.
Result
Detected 12 mm of unexpected differential settlement in one pier foundation three months before the client’s own inspection schedule. Structural intervention carried out proactively.
Sector 02 — Environmental
Nairobi, Kenya
Land-cover change monitoring, East African dry forest corridor
TerraTech Nairobi processes a 10-year Sentinel-2 time series across a 2,400 km² dry forest corridor on behalf of a conservation finance fund. Monthly change detection runs on TerraCloud. When deforestation events are detected, alerts go to both the client and local rangers within 48 hours. Carbon stock estimates feed into REDD+ reporting.
Result
Early detection rate for illegal clearing events improved from quarterly reviews to 48-hour automated alerts. Annual carbon stock variance reduced to ±3%.
Sector 03 — Mining
Perth, Australia
Open-pit volume reconciliation, iron ore mine, Pilbara region
TerraTech Perth conducts monthly drone LiDAR surveys across a 340-hectare open-pit iron ore mine. Sub-centimetre digital terrain models are compared epoch-to-epoch to produce extraction volume reconciliation reports. Results are overlaid with blast block models to verify that declared tonnages match spatial reality.
Result
Volume reconciliation accuracy of ±0.4% against plant weighbridge data. Replaced a manual total-station survey process that took 3 days per cycle with a 6-hour drone operation.
Technology

From orbit
to ground truth.

Our position near Kennedy Space Center is not a coincidence. Commercial satellite operators, NASA ground stations, and remote sensing data infrastructure cluster around the KSC corridor. Physical proximity means faster data pipelines, easier calibration validation, and direct access to ground-truth sites used for satellite sensor validation.

TerraTech has maintained a data-sharing agreement with NASA Applied Sciences since 2014 and works with Maxar, Planet Labs, and Airbus Defence & Space to access imagery for any project on earth.

Why Cape Canaveral matters operationally
Satellite operators maintain ground receiving stations near launch sites to reduce latency. Being adjacent to KSC gives TerraTech access to tasked imagery within hours of acquisition — not days. We also participate in NASA’s Cal/Val programmes, which means our ground surveys are used to validate the accuracy of orbital sensors. That calibration relationship gives us preferential data access and early access to new sensor platforms before commercial release.

Life at TerraTech

What it actually
feels like to work here.

TerraTech is not a typical consultancy. Most senior staff joined as graduates and have been here for over a decade — that tells you something about the culture. Teams are small (4–8 people per project), flat in structure, and technically serious. A junior surveyor who disagrees with a methodology choice is expected to say so — with evidence.

Field travel is real. If you are in the Perth office working on mining contracts, expect to spend time in the Pilbara. If you are in Madrid on an infrastructure project, you may be in Colombia the following month. This is not fine print — it is central to the job and one of the main reasons people choose TerraTech over a desk-bound GIS role.

Team sizes
Projects run with 4–8 people. One named Project Geodesist, one Project Manager, the rest technical specialists. You know the project end-to-end rather than owning one small step.
Graduate Programme
18-month rotation across field survey, GIS analysis, and remote sensing teams. You graduate with a broad technical foundation, then specialise. Mentored by a Senior Geodesist throughout.
Field travel
Significant. Varies by office and role. Perth mining contracts can mean 6–8 weeks in the Pilbara per year. Madrid infrastructure projects involve regular travel to project sites across the EU and Latin America.
Open-source culture
We use QGIS, GDAL, PostGIS, and Python as standard. We contribute to open datasets. You will not be locked into proprietary tools or writing reports no one reads. TerraCloud is where the work lives.

Interview Preparation
Before you
speak to Alex.

Your interviewer is Alex Chen, Senior HR Manager. He has conducted over 400 interviews. He can tell immediately whether you have read this page or not. Here is what research actually looks like in the room:

01
Know which of the three sectors interests you — and have a genuine reason, not a generic one. “Environmental monitoring because of climate reporting requirements” is specific. “I like geospatial work” is not.
02
Understand what TerraCloud does and why it changes the client experience. The answer is: live monitoring vs. periodic PDF reports. Know why that matters to a mining operator or environmental fund.
03
Know why the Cape Canaveral location matters operationally. The answer is: proximity to satellite ground stations, faster data pipelines, and participation in NASA calibration/validation programmes.
04
Have one specific project or technical experience ready as a story. Not “I have done field surveys.” A specific situation, what you did, and what the outcome was.
05
Be ready to name one skill you are actively developing and what you are concretely doing about it. “I am learning Python for spatial analysis and I am currently working through a QGIS automation project” is the level of specificity Alex expects.
